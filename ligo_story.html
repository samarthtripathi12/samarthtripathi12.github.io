Project II — Research Story

Extracting Signal
from Spacetime Noise:
LIGO GW150914

A computational signal processing pipeline applied to real LIGO H1 strain data — Fourier transforms derived from definition, Butterworth band-pass filtering, and SNR detection for the first observed gravitational wave event.

DomainGravitational Wave Signal Processing
DataLIGO H1 — GW150914
Filter4th-Order Butterworth · 50–600 Hz
StartedAugust 2023
Section 01

The Intellectual Trigger

GW150914 was announced in February 2016. The signal — two black holes merging 1.3 billion light-years away — lasted less than half a second in the LIGO detector data. What I found remarkable was not the detection itself but what it required computationally: the signal was smaller than the noise by a factor that made direct observation impossible. The raw strain data shows nothing recognizable. The chirp only appears after processing.

I wanted to understand what that processing actually does. The papers described matched filtering, power spectral density estimation, and template banks. But I wanted to begin earlier — at the Fourier transform itself, the step that converts a time-domain signal into something where filtering is possible. The question that bothered me was specific: why does filtering in the frequency domain correspond to anything meaningful in the time domain? The convolution theorem provides the formal answer, but I had never worked through it in a real signal context where the noise structure was physical rather than synthetic.

The second question was about the noise itself. Broadband detector noise is not arbitrary. It has a known spectral shape — seismic at low frequencies, shot noise at high frequencies, thermal noise in between. I wanted to know whether computing that structure from the data itself, and building a filter around it, would reveal the chirp. That is what I set out to test.

Section 02

First Principles Derivation

I began with the Fourier transform as a definition, not a tool. The continuous form expresses a signal as a superposition of complex exponentials at every frequency — each with an amplitude and a phase. The derivation starts from the requirement that the exponentials form an orthogonal basis over the real line, which determines the normalization and the inversion formula.

Continuous Fourier Transform — from orthogonal basis argument
s̃(f) = ∫₋∞^∞ s(t) e^{−2πift} dt
Inversion: s(t) = ∫₋∞^∞ s̃(f) e^{2πift} df

Moving to the discrete case required careful attention to the sampling theorem. LIGO H1 data is sampled at 4096 Hz, so the maximum representable frequency is 2048 Hz. The discrete Fourier transform maps N time-domain samples to N frequency-domain bins, with a frequency resolution of fs/N. I derived the DFT from the continuous definition by approximating the integral as a Riemann sum over the sampling interval — this makes the relationship between resolution and record length concrete rather than formal.

For the filter, I derived the ideal band-pass transfer function: unity within the signal band (50–600 Hz), zero outside it. This is an ideal filter that is not physically realizable because its time-domain equivalent is non-causal. In practice I used a 4th-order Butterworth filter, whose passband is maximally flat and whose roll-off I computed from the pole locations in the s-plane. The zero-phase implementation using forward-backward filtering effectively doubles the filter order to 8, which I verified by checking the phase response — it should be identically zero across all frequencies.

Section 03

The Technical Failures

Failure 01 — Spectral Leakage

What went wrong: My first FFT of the raw strain data produced a spectrum with large spurious peaks at frequencies unrelated to any known noise source. The spectrum looked structured, but the structure did not correspond to the expected noise floor shape.

Why it went wrong: The 32-second data segment I was using did not begin and end at the same amplitude. The DFT treats the signal as if it were periodic — when the endpoints do not match, this introduces an artificial discontinuity that spreads energy across all frequency bins. This is spectral leakage, and it can make a broadband noise floor appear to have sharp features that do not exist in the physical signal.

How I diagnosed it: I plotted the raw time-series endpoints. They were different by a small but non-negligible amount. I then compared my spectrum against published LIGO noise curves from the O1 observing run — the shape did not match. The mismatch at low frequencies was the giveaway: real seismic noise has a smooth power-law fall-off, but my spectrum had jagged artifacts below 50 Hz.

What I changed: I applied a Tukey window to the time-series before computing the FFT. The window tapers the signal to zero at both endpoints smoothly, eliminating the discontinuity. The resulting spectrum matched the expected noise floor shape, and the low-frequency artifacts disappeared.

Failure 02 — Filter Phase Distortion

What went wrong: My first filtered output had the right frequency content but the chirp was displaced in time relative to the raw signal. The merger peak appeared at the wrong moment.

Why it went wrong: I had implemented the Butterworth filter using a forward-only pass. Standard IIR filters introduce frequency-dependent phase shifts — lower frequencies are delayed more than higher ones. In a chirp signal that sweeps from low to high frequency, this produces a smeared, time-shifted output rather than a clean waveform.

What I changed: I switched to zero-phase filtering using SciPy's filtfilt — forward pass followed immediately by a reversed backward pass. The two-pass approach cancels all phase shifts exactly, at the cost of doubling the effective filter order. The chirp peak aligned correctly with the raw data timeline after this change, and the merger structure became visually clean.

Failure 03 — SNR Proxy Interpretation

What went wrong: My initial SNR calculation showed peaks at multiple times in the 32-second window, not just at the merger. I was uncertain whether any of them represented the real signal.

Why it went wrong: I had normalized the filtered signal amplitude by the RMS over the full 32 seconds. But the noise amplitude is not stationary — it varies across the record. Periods of elevated noise (seismic transients) produced local amplitude peaks that looked like signal when divided by a global RMS baseline.

What I changed: I computed the RMS in a sliding window rather than globally, then inspected the highest-amplitude event in the result. The peak at ~16 seconds into the record was isolated, had the right frequency-time structure visible in the spectrogram, and corresponded to the known merger time of GW150914 within the data segment. This confirmed the detection.

Section 04

Conceptual Realization

The insight I arrived at through this project is about the nature of noise itself.

Detector noise is not random chaos. It has structure — a spectral shape determined by physical mechanisms. The filter does not remove noise. It reshapes the probability distribution of what remains.

Before building this pipeline, I thought of filtering as subtraction — as removing the noise and leaving the signal. That framing is wrong. The Butterworth filter does not know which frequency components are noise and which are signal. It passes everything in the 50–600 Hz band and attenuates everything outside it. What makes this useful is that the gravitational wave signal from a binary merger happens to live primarily in that band, while the dominant noise sources — seismic and suspension thermal noise — are concentrated below 50 Hz, and shot noise dominates above 600 Hz.

The filter works not because it separates signal from noise but because it selects a frequency region where the signal-to-noise ratio is more favorable. After filtering, the noise that remains is still physical detector noise, not some residual artifact. The chirp appears not because noise was removed but because the noise that was removed was much louder than the noise that was left, and the signal is comparable in amplitude to the noise that remains.

Conceptual Insight

This reframing has a consequence for how I interpret the SNR. The amplitude-to-RMS ratio I computed is not a measure of how clearly the signal stands above a noise-free background — it is a measure of how much the signal stands above the remaining noise after frequency selection. A higher SNR proxy means the signal amplitude is large relative to the in-band noise floor, not that the noise has been eliminated.

Full matched filtering goes further: it uses a template of the expected waveform to correlate against the data across all possible arrival times and masses. That technique, which this project does not implement, can recover signals buried below the in-band noise floor. What I demonstrated is the step before that — that the chirp structure is visible at all after band-pass filtering, which it is.

Section 05

Limitations & Intellectual Humility

This pipeline demonstrates the signal processing concepts underlying gravitational wave detection. It is not a detection pipeline in the operational LIGO sense, and the gap between the two is significant.

  • No matched filtering is implemented. The real LIGO detection of GW150914 used template banks of post-Newtonian waveforms correlated against the data across all possible binary masses, mass ratios, and arrival times. This project uses only band-pass filtering and amplitude thresholding, which is a much weaker detection method.
  • The SNR proxy uses amplitude divided by in-band RMS — this is not the optimal statistic. Optimal matched-filter SNR weights each frequency bin by the inverse of the noise power spectral density, which requires independent PSD estimation from off-signal data. That step is not performed here.
  • No false-alarm probability is computed. A detection claim requires knowing the probability that noise alone could produce a peak as large as the observed one, which requires running the same pipeline on many segments of background noise. This project does not perform that analysis.
  • The analysis uses a single detector (H1). The real GW150914 detection used both LIGO observatories simultaneously, with the signal arriving at Hanford 7 milliseconds before Livingston. Time-coincidence between detectors is a critical noise rejection step not present here.
  • Spectral leakage mitigation used a Tukey window chosen by inspection. A rigorous choice would involve comparing window functions and selecting based on the sidelobe structure relevant to the signal bandwidth.

Section 06

Why This Project Changed How I Think

This project changed how I think about measurement. Before it, I understood that signals are noisy — that any physical measurement contains error. What I did not appreciate is that noise is itself a physical quantity with a structure that tells you something about the detector.

The LIGO noise floor is not arbitrary. Its shape below 50 Hz reflects the suspension system isolating the mirrors from ground motion. Its shape above 600 Hz reflects the quantum shot noise of the laser photons. The thermal noise that dominates in between reflects the mechanical properties of the mirror coatings. Every feature of the noise spectrum is an engineering and physics fact about the instrument.

Before filtering, the data contains both the gravitational wave and information about the detector. After filtering, you have selected a frequency window where the physics you want is louder than the physics of the instrument.

That reframing — noise as information about the instrument, not just contamination — is the conceptual shift I carried out of this project. It applies broadly: in any measurement, the noise spectrum is a model of what the measuring device is sensitive to. Understanding the noise is not a prerequisite for analysis; it is part of the analysis. The two cannot be separated cleanly, and this project made that concrete in a way that a textbook description of the Wiener filter had not.
